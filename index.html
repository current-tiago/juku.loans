An interest rate swap lets two businesses exchange their loan payment structures — without refinancing. It's one of the most common financial instruments in the world, now accessible to SMEs.

The two parties
Party A
The fixed-rate seeker
Has a variable-rate loan (e.g. linked to SONIA). Wants certainty — a predictable monthly payment for budgeting.
Pays fixed, receives float
Party B
The float-rate seeker
Has a fixed-rate loan. Believes rates will fall and wants to benefit — willing to take on variability in exchange.
Pays float, receives fixed

Neither party changes their underlying loan. They simply exchange the interest payment streams — the net difference changes hands each period.

How it works, step by step
1

Both parties have existing loans

Party A has a £500k variable-rate loan at SONIA + 2%. Party B has a £500k fixed-rate loan at 6.5%. Each is unhappy with their structure for their own reasons.

2

They agree a notional and a swap rate

The notional amount is the reference figure (e.g. £500k) — no principal is exchanged, ever. They agree a fixed swap rate (e.g. 5.5%) against a floating benchmark like SONIA.

3

Each period, only the net difference is paid

If SONIA is 4.5%, the floating rate is 4.5%. The fixed payer owes 5.5%; the float payer owes 4.5%. The fixed payer pays the net 1% difference — not both full amounts.

4

Each party effectively converts their loan

Party A now has predictable costs — their variable loan plus the fixed swap nets out to approximately the fixed rate. Party B gains exposure to a falling rate environment.

Common questions
No. The notional is just a reference number used to calculate the interest payments. No principal is exchanged at any point in a standard interest rate swap.
In the UK, SONIA (Sterling Overnight Index Average) replaced LIBOR as the standard floating benchmark in 2021. It reflects the rate banks lend to each other overnight.
Both parties take on rate risk in opposite directions. If rates rise above the swap rate, Party A benefits (their fixed payment is below market). If rates fall, Party B benefits.
